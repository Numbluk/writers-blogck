title: "My Goal is to Leave Tech, And It Should Be Yours Too"
---

I've been in tech for almost ten years now. I'm an outlier by almost every standard because I've remained at the same company since I virtually started my career.
Some would consider this career stagnation, but my ever-growing CV is living proof of the opposite. I say this because it's not like I'm stuck. In fact, I'm lucky
to be at a company that rewards growth, so in some respects the sky is the limit if I stay.

But I won't. 

Like every do-gooder software engineer, the optimistic lights of rocketships and unicorns kept my eyes on the prize:. In my mind, I was getting paid to become an expert
where the skill ceiling was unlimited. On top of that I had status and money thrown into the mix. My purpose was to grow and by extension help the company grow--what a deal!

I've decided to artificially limit my tech career. My exit date is the 24th of July, 2037 or roughly 12 years from now. While stressful, it is invigorating to think that
there will be some end to this mess.

## Why?

To put it simply: I have come to believe that my corporate contributions are a net negative on society.

Although I am an engineering manager, I still consider myself a software engineer at heart. Building something with my "bare hands" and seeing electricity shock my creation
into life still keeps me interested, but my day-to-day is not this reality. Instead, I look on in horror at Dr. Frankenstein's creature which we have unleashed on the world.
I look at how this corrodes and sexualizes the minds of our children, destroys relationships, breaks down families, murders innovation, and drives up housing prices and kills off
multi-generational family bonds and local culture.

Growing up, there used to be a joke about how if lawyers disappeared the world would be a better place. I have now come to believe that this is actually true for most of the
> "The only real sadness, the only real failure, the only great tragedy in life, is not to become a saint."


I would challenge you to look outside the work in front of you. The common thread of software engineers (and probably most types of engineers) whom I know is problem solving. 

The more I've longer I've worked in this industry the more I've come to realise how difficult it is to do good. In my line of work, my ability to do good is severely limited to
those around me and the money I can give away. I'm an engineering manager and my style is to be as thoroughly honest as possible. I've accepted that this is not everyone's cup of tea and
that it will probably hamper my career trajectory, however, I cannot bear to see those in my care struggle because I try to spin every piece of work and every company message in an
impossible golden light. 

But what about harm? 

Purpose at work is a fickle thing; it's always out of reach, just beyond the Pale. "Do what you're good at, not what you love." Check. All is well with my soul, so no purpose needed.
But what about harm?

## How?

And when I say "exit", I truly mean it. I'm not talking about a tech-adjacent career where I'm consulting or creating mentoring, no, I'm all in on the clean break.

This is both easy and difficult. I don't live a life of luxury. Even with young mouths to feed and a mortgage to pay, I live below my means. 

## \*Conditions

If my life gets financially upended and I'm forced to put food on the table and my only option to realistically do that is through Tech then I will do so.
