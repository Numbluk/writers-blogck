title: "AI Makes The Hard Parts of Delivering Software Harder"
date: 2026-03-14
draft: true
---

Before the dawn of LLMs, software projects always had an absorbption factor.
an engineer would scope a project based on their understanding of a code base and their familiarity with a domain. The project lifecycle would always include
unknown comlexity typically obfuscated by unknown dependencies. An engineering's planning made them act as a minesweeper: lift up boulders, comb through
the earth and disarm along the way. But an engineer was never a full-time minesweeper. The work istelf allowed some suprises to blow up in your
face and the project would absorb it because the project required time to deliver as code.
While code itself was rarely the true hurdle, it still took time. And it was that time spent writing code that allowed you to sort these surprises in parallel.
Now that this is gone, I am a full-time minesweeper.

And it was this time spent writing code that allowed an engineer to resolve these surprises in parallel. In between design patterns, tests and attempts at coherent taste, messages
are sent, meetings are held and consensus or tradeoffs made to deliver all on time. These blockers were the things which cause the most anxiety:

> A team can't deliver X by such-and-such date, so we must scale back on delivery goals--we'll push it to phase two. Or, a new way of provisioning
> infrastructure secrets is tied to a manual gatekeepr--but by whom? Which engineer out of thousands and in what timezone? Ah! The team is found, but
> they only respond when it's 8:00 PM and I'm just putting the kids to bed. That's fine, I'll sacrifice it just this once. Actually, I'll keep this
> going as long as I can. At last, it's been ten years since, so maybe I'll stick it out until I can retire early.

Take this experience. I know many of us have lived it. And we know it's never just once. Project after project, the pattern of human
and corporate bodies reassemble themselves into monstrosities that delay us and fight us tooth and nail until we reach the bitter end and start all
over again with the next one.

Now, these most painful parts are about to get worse. Not because the problems are more complex or tediously boring, but because there are more of them
to deal with at once. I believe the next phase of our industry will try to strip away the time-consuming and unecessary processes away from humans
and offload them to AI, but nothing is ever free.

What some "condescendingly" call automation, I call freedom. Freedom away from old drudgery and freedom to be creative. But
in the midst of this code storm we will find that old problems remain and critical thinking will become mor evaluable than all because driving
solutions through corporate bodies is a skill unto itself and it isn't somthing you wake up doing. Even with all the creativity in the world, if you
can't convince someone to use it or buy into it then it's simply not valuable.
